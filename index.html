The Tech Neck Panic: A Billion-Dollar Hoax

The Evidence Is Overwhelming

A 2023 scoping review analyzed 41 studies on tech neck. While "tech neck" is frequently defined as a flexed neck position, there is no scientific evidence linking this posture to neck pain. The researchers recommended scientists stop using judgmental adjectives like "incorrect" when describing these postures, as the link to tissue damage remains unproven.

Study after study confirms this:

  • Bertozzi et al. (2021): 238 university students. Result? No significant correlations between hours spent on smartphones or posture during use and neck pain.
  • Correia et al. (2021): 582 adults measured with precise devices. Finding? Neck flexion angle was not associated with the prevalence, frequency, or intensity of neck pain.
  • Damasceno et al. (2018): 150 young adults. Conclusion? No association between neck posture and neck pain.

This isn't one fluke study. This is a systematic pattern across multiple countries and thousands of participants.

Even Lab Experiments Failed to Create the Problem

Kim et al. (2024) had 17 people maintain a 30-degree forward head tilt for 30 minutes straight‚Äîfar more extreme than typical phone use. While participants reported mild discomfort, objective measures of cervical spine stability did not change significantly.

A 2025 scoping review applied Hill's Criteria of Causation‚Äîthe gold standard for establishing if something causes disease‚Äîto 21 articles on tech neck. The relationship satisfied none of the criteria for causality.

The Sitting "Epidemic" That Never Was

"Sitting is the new smoking!"

Maybe you bought a standing desk because of it. The narrative is everywhere: Our sedentary lifestyle is destroying our spines.

Except occupational sitting doesn't cause low back pain.

A 2010 systematic review examined 24 studies using rigorous Bradford-Hill criteria for causation. Their conclusion? Strong, consistent evidence for no association between occupational sitting and low back pain.

A 2025 meta-analysis of 129 reports found the same pattern. When researchers followed people over time to see if sitting caused pain, the relationship vanished. Sedentary behavior does not appear to cause spinal pain in young people.

Does sitting all day affect your circulation and metabolism? Yes. But the claim that your sitting posture is wrecking your spine? Not supported by evidence.

The Slouch That Protects You

Here's where it gets weird.

What if "good" posture is actually worse for you than slouching?

The Study That Flipped Everything: Researchers tracked 686 participants from age 17 to 22. For females, compared to those who sat in an "upright" posture, those with a "slumped thorax/forward head" position had decreased odds of persistent neck pain (OR = 0.24).

Translation: Slouching was protective. Sitting up straight increased risk.

The researchers were so struck by this that they concluded: "The practice of generic public health messages to sit up straight to prevent neck pain needs rethinking."

The advice drilled into us since childhood‚Äîsit up straight to avoid pain‚Äîmay actually be making things worse.

Your "Abnormal" Spine Is Normal

"But my doctor said my curved spine is why my back hurts."

People show me X-rays with spinal curves, disc degeneration, asymmetries. They've been told these "problems" cause their pain.

Here's what research shows:

A 2006 study compared 54 people with neck pain to 53 without it, using radiographs to measure spinal curvature. The study found no significant difference in spinal curvature between the groups. Structural abnormalities appeared equally in both groups.

Your spine can look "bad" on an X-ray and feel perfectly fine. Or look "perfect" and hurt terribly.

What the Research Actually Shows

Lederman's comprehensive review examined decades of research on the Postural-Structural-Biomechanical model. The findings:

  • No association between spinal asymmetry, kyphosis, and lordosis in teenagers and future low back pain
  • Extent of lordosis and presence of scoliosis don't correlate with back pain
  • Pelvic asymmetry shows no correlation with lower back pain
  • Leg length inequality isn't clinically significant until it reaches 20+ millimeters

Even disc degeneration doesn't predict pain. X-ray and MRI findings have no predictive value for future low back pain or disability.

The "abnormalities" doctors point to on imaging studies are found just as often in people without pain. They're not abnormalities‚Äîthey're normal human variation.

Genes Matter More Than Posture: Research shows 47-66% of spinal degeneration is due to hereditary and environmental factors, while only 2-10% can be explained by physical stresses from work or sports.

Where "Good Posture" Really Comes From

If posture doesn't cause pain, why are we obsessed with it?

"Good posture" was invented to enforce military discipline and social class distinctions.

Historian Sander Gilman traced the origins: The modern Western ideal of the "upright, straight-backed" stance comes from 17th-century military drill manuals. Soldiers needed to stand at attention to load muskets in unison. This rigid position became the standard for "normal" and "healthy" carriage.

By the 18th century, "posture masters" taught the upper classes these "artificial contortions."

It gets darker. In the 19th century, upright posture was used to distinguish "advanced" from "primitive" peoples. "Bad" posture was linked to moral failure. Slouching meant weak will or moral decay.

The reason you were told to sit up straight wasn't based on physiology. It was based on centuries of social control, military conformity, and pseudoscientific racism.

You don't have "bad posture." You have a normal human body judged by an arbitrary standard rooted in authoritarian discipline.
